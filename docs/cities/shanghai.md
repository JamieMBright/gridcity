Shanghai's colour character is a humid two-tone city: low-saturation greys and creams (old brick, white tile, river silt) under a soft hazy sky, set against bursts of jade-and-bronze glass and lacquered red temple-work. Golden hour reads as a milky, diffused orange — haze flattens shadows and gilds the curtain-glass — and after dark Lujiazui flips to electric magenta, blue and white neon mirrored in the river.

## Architectural character

The everyday fabric is three layers stacked by era: dense rows of two-to-three-storey grey-brick **shikumen** lilong lane houses with carved stone door-frames (the "stone-gate" lintels that name them) and low blue-grey tiled pitched roofs; vast tracts of 1990s–2000s mid-rise apartment slabs sheathed in cream and white ceramic tile with flat or shallow roofs; and the newest glass-and-steel towers. The CBD is split across the Huangpu River into two opposed characters: the west-bank **Bund** is a continuous wall of pale granite and limestone neoclassical colonial facades — domes, columns, clock towers — built low and heavy in the 1920s, while directly across the water **Lujiazui/Pudong** erupts into a tight cluster of glittering supertall glass towers with sculpted crowns. Roof forms therefore range from humble grey-tile gables and Bund cupolas to the tapered pagoda-stack of Jin Mao, the trapezoid void of the World Financial Center, and the soft helical twist of Shanghai Tower. Shanghai is instantly recognisable from that river panorama: the spheres-on-a-stalk **Oriental Pearl**, the "bottle-opener" notch of the **World Financial Center**, and the gently corkscrewing **Shanghai Tower** form a skyline silhouette no other city shares.

## Heroes (ranked)

1. **Oriental Pearl Tower**
   - Type: tower (TV/observation)
   - Height: 468 m (top floor 351 m; antenna adds 137 m)
   - Recreate-it spec: A tripod of three splayed concrete legs rising to a central vertical shaft that is threaded through two large magenta-pink spheres — a big lower sphere and a smaller upper sphere — plus a small top pod, capped by a thin tapering antenna mast. Colour the shaft and legs pinkish-silver and make the spheres glossy magenta. Very tall and slender overall (~8:1 height:width); at night the spheres glow hot pink.

2. **Shanghai Tower**
   - Type: skyscraper
   - Height: 632 m, 128 floors
   - Recreate-it spec: The tallest form in the skyline — a soft, rounded triangular prism that twists roughly 120° as it rises, tapering gently to a rounded top. A second transparent glass skin wraps the inner tower with a continuous vertical seam spiralling up one edge. Render in pale jade-to-silver glass, slightly cooler and more transparent than its neighbours, with a smooth uncrowned summit. Slim and tapering, about 7:1.

3. **Shanghai World Financial Center** (the "bottle opener")
   - Type: skyscraper
   - Height: 492 m to roof (494.3 m to tip), 101 floors
   - Recreate-it spec: A flat blade that tapers from a square base to a thin top edge, like a wide chisel. Its signature is a large rectangular hole (a trapezoidal aperture) punched right through the top — the "bottle-opener" void. Dark blue-green to near-black reflective glass with crisp vertical mullions. Read it as a flat slab, not a square tower; markedly thinner one way than the other.

4. **Jin Mao Tower**
   - Type: skyscraper
   - Height: 420.5 m, 88 floors
   - Recreate-it spec: A pagoda reinterpreted in steel and glass — a tapering tower built of stacked tiered setbacks that grow shorter and closer together toward the top, ending in a slender finial spire. Silvery aluminium and warm-grey glass with strong vertical ribs catching gold at sunset. Classical, symmetrical, lace-like detail near the crown; proportions about 6:1.

5. **The Bund (waterfront ensemble)**
   - Type: civic (historic district)
   - Height: mostly 6–9 storeys; ~1.5 km continuous river frontage
   - Recreate-it spec: A long unbroken row of pale granite and cream-stone neoclassical buildings facing the river — columned porticos, cornices, and a scatter of green-and-grey domes and a clock tower. Keep them low, heavy and horizontal, in dressed grey-buff stone with dark tiled or copper-green roofs. The defining gesture is the continuous stone wall along a broad stone promenade beside muddy brown water.

6. **Custom House ("Big Ching")**
   - Type: civic (with clock tower)
   - Height: eight storeys, topped by a tall square clock-and-bell tower
   - Recreate-it spec: A blocky grey-stone neoclassical building on the Bund with a square clock tower rising from its roofline, a large four-faced clock near the top and a small domed/pyramidal cap above. Pale granite walls, dark roof; the bell tower (nicknamed "Big Ching" after Big Ben) is the silhouette anchor — make it clearly taller than its neighbours.

7. **HSBC Building (the Bund)**
   - Type: civic
   - Height: six floors, crowned by a hemispherical dome
   - Recreate-it spec: A broad, symmetrical neoclassical bank in pale grey granite with a grand columned entrance and, dead-centre on the roof, a low green-grey hemispherical copper dome on a drum. Wide and squat rather than tall; pair of bronze lions could flank the doorway. The dome is the recognisable feature.

8. **Jing'an Temple**
   - Type: religious (Buddhist temple)
   - Height: low complex; main halls 1–2 storeys with tall ornamental roofs
   - Recreate-it spec: A walled compound of Buddhist halls with steeply upturned, sweeping eaves; the roofs are unusually finished in **golden / gilt** tiles rather than grey, blazing against the surrounding glass towers. Deep red timber columns and walls below, ornate bracketed eaves above. Squat footprint, glittering gold rooflines — its incongruous gold-among-skyscrapers setting is the hook.

9. **Yu Garden & City God Temple bazaar**
   - Type: civic (classical garden + temple market)
   - Height: 1–2 storey pavilions; ~2 ha walled garden
   - Recreate-it spec: A cluster of small classical-Chinese pavilions with grey-tile upturned eaves, whitewashed walls topped by undulating "dragon" wall-copings, ponds, zig-zag bridges and a five-sided lake teahouse. Grey roofs, white walls, red lattice windows and lanterns. Dense, intricate and low — a maze of little roofs, not a single building.

10. **China Pavilion (Expo 2010, "Oriental Crown")**
    - Type: pavilion
    - Height: 63 m (tallest Expo structure)
    - Recreate-it spec: A giant inverted pyramid / stepped crown sitting on four massive square legs, formed of stacked horizontal beams that cantilever outward and upward like a traditional dougong bracket. Paint it in bold layered **Chinese reds** (seven subtle shades). Wider at the top than the bottom — an unmistakable upside-down stepped crown floating on legs.

11. **Lupu Bridge**
    - Type: bridge (steel through-arch)
    - Height: arch rises ~100 m above the deck; 550 m main span, 3,900 m total
    - Recreate-it spec: A single huge steel through-arch leaping over the river, the curved arch ribs rising high above a flat roadway deck slung beneath them on near-vertical hangers. Slender steel arch painted pale grey/white, a long shallow rainbow curve. The deck is straight and level; the great arc above it is the silhouette.

12. **Nanpu Bridge**
    - Type: bridge (cable-stayed)
    - Height: H-shaped pylons ~150 m; 428 m main span
    - Recreate-it spec: A cable-stayed span with tall H-shaped towers and fans of cables. Its famous feature is the approach on the Puxi side: the roadway spirals down in a tight complete loop (a helix) to meet the ground. Grey concrete towers, harp/fan cable arrays; draw the corkscrew approach ramp as the giveaway detail.

13. **Yangpu Bridge**
    - Type: bridge (cable-stayed)
    - Height: diamond/A-shaped pylons ~220 m; 602 m main span; 8,354 m total
    - Recreate-it spec: A very long cable-stayed bridge with two tall A-frame (diamond) pylons and broad fans of cables splaying down to a high deck. Bigger and longer than its sister Nanpu; the A-shaped towers and the sheer span length are the recognisable traits. Pale concrete towers, light cables.

14. **Tomorrow Square (JW Marriott)**
    - Type: skyscraper
    - Height: 284.6 m, 60 floors
    - Recreate-it spec: A tower whose four corners are chamfered and which rotates 45° partway up so the upper shaft sits diagonally on the lower one. The crown splits into four pointed prongs that splay outward like a claw or rocket fins around a central spike. Pale silver-grey glass and metal; futuristic, jagged top — the four-pronged claw crown is the signature.

15. **Park Hotel**
    - Type: skyscraper (Art Deco hotel)
    - Height: ~83.8 m, 22 storeys above ground (tallest in Asia 1934–1963)
    - Recreate-it spec: A slim Art Deco tower with strong vertical lines and stepped setbacks near the top. The lowest three floors are polished black granite; the shaft above is dark brown brick and brown ceramic tile, capping in receding stepped tiers. Narrow, dark and vertical — a 1930s Manhattan-style ziggurat profile in brown and black.

16. **Wukang Mansion**
    - Type: civic (historic apartment block, flatiron)
    - Height: eight storeys
    - Recreate-it spec: A flatiron building on a sharp street corner — a narrow wedge with a rounded prow, like a ship's bow. Cream-and-rust render with red brick, French-Renaissance balconies and arched ground-floor arcades. Eight floors, tapering acute plan; the curved triangular nose pointing down the street fork is the whole identity.

17. **Shanghai Grand Theatre**
    - Type: civic (opera house)
    - Height: low-rise civic block, ~40 m
    - Recreate-it spec: A glass-and-white cube whose flat roof curves sharply upward at its four corners into a concave, sky-facing sweep — like a crescent or a pair of upturned wings catching rain. Translucent white glass walls glowing from within at night, crisp white frame. The up-curled "moon-cradle" roof is the recognisable move.

18. **Longhua Temple & Pagoda**
    - Type: pagoda (with temple complex)
    - Height: pagoda ~40 m, seven octagonal storeys (oldest landmark in Shanghai)
    - Recreate-it spec: A slender seven-storey octagonal brick pagoda; each tier has projecting upturned timber-eaves with little corner bells and a small balcony rail, narrowing as it rises to a thin spire finial. Reddish-brown brick core with dark grey-tile eaves and ochre woodwork. Beside it, low grey-tile temple halls with red walls. The tapering many-eaved tower is the focal point.

19. **Mercedes-Benz Arena**
    - Type: stadium (indoor arena)
    - Height: low domed disc; seats ~18,000
    - Recreate-it spec: A wide, low circular building shaped like a flattened flying saucer — a shallow silver-white disc with a broad overhanging rim and a domed centre, lit with coloured LED edge-banding. Sits low and round; the UFO/flying-disc profile distinguishes it from a boxy stadium.

20. **Shanghai Railway Station**
    - Type: station
    - Height: long low terminal block with a raised central span
    - Recreate-it spec: A long horizontal 1980s terminal — a wide flat-roofed concourse with a taller glazed central hall, a big clock and signage over the entrance, and platform sheds behind. Pale concrete and grey-blue glass, strongly horizontal, a broad forecourt in front. Reads as a big civic transit slab rather than an ornate landmark.
